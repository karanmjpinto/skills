# Signature Slide Frameworks

These two slides are what distinguish a Studio deck from a generic pitch. They demonstrate structural thinking to co-investors and signal that the investment thesis is grounded in first principles, not trend-chasing.

---

## 1. Global Context Slide

### Purpose

Establish three first-principles truths that any sophisticated investor will agree with *before* hearing anything about the company. This slide sets the intellectual frame — it says "the world is moving this way, and our company is built on that movement."

### The Four Tests

Every force on this slide must pass ALL four:

1. **Directional** — it points somewhere; it doesn't just describe a state
2. **Irreversible** — no reasonable person would argue it goes the other way
3. **Structural** — driven by technology, economics, or human behaviour — not trend, hype, or policy
4. **Privately connected** to one of the company's specific differentiators, but not stated explicitly on this slide

If a candidate force fails any test, reject it. Trend-flavoured forces ("AI is hot right now") weaken the slide and invite skepticism from sophisticated investors.

### Three Domains

The three forces must come from different domains:

| Force | Domain | Lens |
|-------|--------|------|
| **Force 01** | How the core technology or product category works | Technology / product dynamics |
| **Force 02** | Where or how it must be delivered | Distribution / delivery model |
| **Force 03** | How value is created and captured | Economics / value capture |

### Content Structure Per Force

For each of the three forces, produce:

**Headline**: `[Subject] is [verb]-ing →`
- The arrow signals direction, not description
- Keep under 8 words
- Example: "Compute is commoditising →" or "Distribution is fragmenting →"

**Why it exists and cannot reverse** (2–3 sentences):
- The cause must be structural, not cyclical or sentiment-driven
- Reference underlying technology shifts, economic gravity, or behavioural patterns
- This is the "physics" of the argument

**What it structurally demands** (2–3 sentences):
- Format: "Any company that wants to win here must..."
- This is the bridge between the force and the business
- Write carefully — this is where the implicit connection to the company lives

**One anchor stat**:
- Specific and citable
- Makes the force feel real and quantifiable
- Vague stats ("the market is big") weaken the slide

**One-line differentiator label** (at the base of the column):
- Subtle — reads like a footnote, not a sales claim
- Format: `[Company]'s answer — [specific capability relevant to this force]`

### Slide-Level Elements

**Slide title**: Short, confident, slightly provocative. Not a question. Not a description. Signals: "this is already settled."
- Good: "Three forces that aren't going back"
- Bad: "Industry Trends" or "Why Now?"

**Footer**: `[Company] is not a [category people put you in]. We are [what you actually are] — built on these three truths.`

### Visual Layout

Three equal columns, one per force. Clean, structured, with clear visual hierarchy:
- Force number and headline at top (large, bold)
- Body text in the middle
- Anchor stat as a callout (large number with source)
- Differentiator label at bottom (smaller, muted)

### After Generating — Show Your Thinking

Include in speaker notes or a separate commentary:
- What forces were considered and rejected? Why?
- Why are these three structurally irreversible, not just likely?
- Which differentiator does each force connect to?
- Flag any force that feels like trend rather than structure — these need hardening before presenting

### Common Mistakes

- **Choosing forces that are just "industry trends"** — "AI adoption is accelerating" fails the structural test. Better: "Intelligence is becoming an infrastructure layer →" (driven by compute economics, not sentiment)
- **All three forces from the same domain** — if all three are about technology, the slide feels one-dimensional
- **Making the connection to the company too obvious** — this slide should make investors nod and think "yes, obviously" before the company is even introduced. The connection should click later, not here.
- **Vague anchor stats** — "$X trillion market" stats are lazy. Find the stat that makes the force *feel* real.

## 2. Growth Flywheel Slide

### Purpose

Show how the business scales with compounding advantages. This isn't a go-to-market slide (that comes later) — it's a *mechanics* slide that answers: "Why does this business get harder to compete with over time?"

The flywheel connects the business model, GTM choices, and defensible moats into a self-reinforcing loop that investors can see will compound.

### Core Framework

A growth flywheel has three components that must be visually and logically connected:

#### A. The Engine (Business Model → Growth)

How the business model itself drives growth:
- What action generates revenue?
- How does each unit of revenue create the conditions for more revenue?
- Where are the unit economics improving with scale?

Example: "Each new enterprise customer generates usage data that improves model accuracy, which drives higher retention and expansion revenue."

#### B. The Moats (What Compounds)

Defensible advantages that deepen with each turn of the flywheel:

| Moat Type | What Compounds | Example |
|-----------|---------------|---------|
| **Data moat** | Proprietary data grows with usage | Each transaction trains the model |
| **Network effects** | Value increases with participants | More suppliers → more buyers → more suppliers |
| **Switching costs** | Integration depth locks in customers | Embedded in workflow, training invested |
| **Scale economies** | Cost advantages widen with volume | Infrastructure cost per unit drops |
| **Brand/trust** | Reputation compounds over time | Regulated industry where trust = barrier |
| **Ecosystem lock-in** | Third-party integrations create stickiness | Developers build on the platform |

Identify which moats are *already forming* vs. which are *projected*. Investors discount projected moats heavily. Be honest.

#### C. The GTM Reinforcement (How Growth Feeds Distribution)

How the go-to-market strategy connects back into the flywheel:
- Does each customer acquisition reduce the cost of the next?
- Does product usage generate distribution (virality, word of mouth, content)?
- Do partnerships create compounding access to new segments?

### Visual Structure

The flywheel should be a **circular or cyclical diagram** — not a linear process flow. Key design principles:

- **3–5 nodes** in the cycle (more than 5 makes it unreadable)
- **Arrows connecting each node** to the next, forming a closed loop
- **Moat labels** attached to the connections between nodes (the moats are what make the cycle *accelerate*, not just repeat)
- **One callout** at the centre or side: the key metric that proves the flywheel is turning (e.g., "NRR: 135%", "CAC declining 15% QoQ")

Example node sequence:
```
Customer Acquisition → Usage & Data Generation → Product Improvement → Higher Retention & Expansion → Referrals & Reputation → Customer Acquisition
```

With moats annotated on the arrows:
```
[Data moat] between Usage and Improvement
[Switching costs] between Improvement and Retention
[Brand/trust] between Retention and Referrals
```

### Content to Include

- **Flywheel narrative** (2–3 sentences): Describe the loop in plain language. "As more enterprises adopt [product], their usage data improves [capability], which drives higher retention and makes the next sale easier and cheaper."
- **Moat evidence**: For each moat claimed, one piece of evidence that it's already forming (not just theoretical)
- **Key metric**: The single number that proves the flywheel is turning
- **Time dimension**: How long has the flywheel been active? Is it early or maturing?

### Speaker Notes Should Address

- Which moats are real today vs. aspirational?
- What could break the flywheel? (shows intellectual honesty)
- How does the funding round accelerate the flywheel?

### Common Mistakes

- **Showing a linear funnel instead of a cycle** — if there's no feedback loop, it's a GTM slide, not a flywheel
- **Too many nodes** — a 7-step flywheel is a process diagram. Keep it to 3–5.
- **Claiming moats that don't exist yet** — investors see through this immediately. Label aspirational moats differently.
- **No proof the flywheel is turning** — without a metric, it's just a theory
- **Confusing the flywheel with the GTM strategy** — the flywheel is about *compounding advantage*, not *how you acquire customers*. GTM is one input to the flywheel, not the whole thing.

## Using Both Slides Together

The Global Context slide (slide 2) sets up the world as it is — three structural forces that demand a certain kind of company. The Growth Flywheel (slide 9) shows that this company has the mechanics to compound advantage within that world.

Together, they create a narrative arc:
1. "The world is moving this way" (Global Context)
2. "Here's what we built for that world" (Problem → Solution → Product → Market → Model → Traction)
3. "Here's why we get stronger over time" (Growth Flywheel)
4. "Here's what we need to accelerate it" (The Ask)

The connection between the Global Context forces and the Flywheel moats should be implicit but unmistakable. Each structural force should map to at least one moat in the flywheel. If they don't connect, either the forces are wrong or the flywheel isn't aligned with the thesis.
