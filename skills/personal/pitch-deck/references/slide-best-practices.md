# Slide-by-Slide Best Practices

This reference contains research-backed guidance for every slide in the deck. Each section explains what investors actually look for, what separates great slides from mediocre ones, and the specific mistakes to avoid.

---

## 1. Title Slide

**Purpose**: First impression. Investors spend 2–5 minutes on an entire deck — the title slide gets maybe 10 seconds. If they can't explain what you do after seeing this slide, you've already lost.

**What great looks like**:
- Company name + one declarative sentence that captures the essence of the business
- The sentence should describe what you DO, not your mission or vision
- Round context if relevant (e.g., "Series A — $8M")
- Clean, confident, uncluttered — logo, tagline, nothing else

**What to avoid**:
- Long mission statements or philosophical positioning
- Too much information (save it for the next slide)
- Generic taglines that could apply to any company ("Revolutionizing the future of X")

**Test**: Can an investor explain your company to their partner in one sentence after seeing this slide?

## 2. Global Context Slide (Studio Signature)

**Purpose**: Establish the structural forces that make this company inevitable — before introducing the company itself. This replaces the traditional "Why Now?" slide with something far more powerful. Instead of "why is this timely," it answers "why is this permanent."

**What great looks like**:
- Three forces from different domains (technology, distribution, economics)
- Each force passes all four tests: directional, irreversible, structural, privately connected
- Headlines use the `[Subject] is [verb]-ing →` format — under 8 words
- Each force has: why it exists and can't reverse, what it structurally demands, one anchor stat, one subtle differentiator label
- Slide title is confident and slightly provocative — signals "this is settled"

**What to avoid**:
- Forces that are just trends or hype ("AI is hot right now")
- All three forces from the same domain
- Making the connection to the company too obvious on this slide
- Vague anchor stats ("$X trillion market")
- Forces that fail the irreversibility test — if a reasonable person could argue it goes the other way, reject it

**What investors think**: "Do these founders understand the structural dynamics of their market, or are they just riding a trend?" This slide separates thesis-driven companies from feature-driven ones.

## 3. Problem Slide

**Purpose**: Make the investor feel the pain — not just understand it intellectually. The problem must be urgent, specific, and validated with data. This is the emotional anchor of the entire deck.

**What great looks like**:
- Describes the pain of the customer in concrete, specific terms — not abstractions
- Uses a real scenario or data point that makes the problem vivid: "Creators with 100,000 followers still can't predict next month's income"
- Shows how the problem is currently addressed and why those solutions fall short
- Quantifies the cost of the problem (time, money, risk, opportunity)
- One key insight: what makes this problem structural, not incidental

**What to avoid**:
- Abstract or generic problem statements: "The current system is inefficient"
- Presenting the problem as obvious without making the investor feel it
- Jumping to the solution on this slide — resist the urge
- Stating your company is the solution here — keep it neutral; describe the type of solution the market needs
- Multiple problems crammed into one slide

**Sequoia's guidance**: "Describe the pain of the customer. Outline how the customer addresses the issue today." Keep it simple, make it visceral.

**Test**: Does this slide make the investor think "yes, that's a real problem and the current solutions aren't good enough"?

## 4. Solution Slide

**Purpose**: Show what changes because of you. Not a product demo — a value proposition. The solution must connect directly back to the specific pain on the previous slide.

**What great looks like**:
- Demonstrates the company's value proposition — how the customer's life gets better
- Directly maps to the problems stated: if the problem was unpredictable income, the solution must fix unpredictability
- Benefits-first language, not features-first: "Access continuously relevant, quality content" not "A social networking oriented Q&A platform"
- Shows where the product physically sits in the customer's workflow
- 2–3 concrete use cases or scenarios

**What to avoid**:
- Technical jargon that loses non-technical investors
- Feature lists instead of benefit descriptions
- Overcomplicating — this slide answers "what do you do and how does it solve the problem," nothing more
- Breaking narrative consistency: the solution must clearly address the problem slide's pain

**Test**: If you cover the company name, could this solution belong to anyone else? If yes, it's not specific enough.

## 5. Product Slide

**Purpose**: Show, don't tell. This is where you get concrete about what you've built — features, architecture, screenshots, demo flow. The solution slide was about the "what," this is about the "how."

**What great looks like**:
- Screenshots, product mockups, or architectural diagrams — visual proof the product exists
- Product line-up: form factor, functionality, key features, IP
- Development roadmap showing what's built vs. what's planned
- If a DD report exists, reference validated technical findings here

**What to avoid**:
- Text-only descriptions of features
- Too much technical detail (save it for the appendix)
- No visual evidence of the product — investors want to see it exists

## 6. Market Opportunity Slide

**Purpose**: Size the prize. Investors need billion-dollar outcome potential. But the sizing must be credible — bottom-up beats top-down every time.

**What great looks like**:
- TAM/SAM/SOM breakdown with clear methodology
- Bottom-up calculation: number of target customers × average revenue per customer = SAM
- SAM should be over $1B for venture-scale opportunities
- Specific customer profile/avatar — who exactly are you selling to?
- Growth rates with sources cited
- Market trends that validate timing

**What to avoid**:
- Top-down only: "$50B industry, we just need 1%" — VCs hate this
- Unsourced numbers or obviously inflated TAMs
- Confusing TAM with SAM — TAM is the total market, SAM is the portion you can actually address
- No customer profile — who is this for, specifically?
- Static market sizing without growth trajectory

**Best practice from funded decks**: The strongest market slides combine a bottom-up SAM calculation with 2–3 growth drivers that explain why the market is expanding now. Cite credible sources (Gartner, CB Insights, government data, industry reports).

## 7. Business Model Slide

**Purpose**: Show how you make money. Keep it simple — investors want to understand the revenue engine in 30 seconds.

**What great looks like**:
- Clear revenue streams: what do you charge for, who pays, and how
- Pricing model that's easy to grasp (subscription tiers, usage-based, transaction fees)
- Key unit economics: LTV, CAC, gross margin, payback period
- For SaaS: ARR, NRR, churn rate
- For marketplaces: take rate, GMV, liquidity metrics

**What to avoid**:
- Overcomplicating with too many revenue streams
- Missing unit economics — investors will ask for them anyway
- No pricing evidence — "we're still figuring out pricing" kills confidence
- Confusing gross and net revenue

**Test**: Can the investor calculate back-of-envelope revenue from this slide? (X customers × $Y per customer = $Z revenue)

## 8. Traction Slide

**Purpose**: Prove momentum — this slide can make or break the entire pitch. Investors want evidence that your thesis is working in the real world. Leading with your best metrics transparently (not buried in appendices) is what separates the top 5% of decks.

**What great looks like**:
- Headline summarizing traction: "50% MoM growth with 10,000 active users"
- A clear growth chart showing trajectory over time (up and to the right)
- Stage-appropriate metrics:
  - Pre-revenue: pilot results, beta users, waitlist, LOIs, partnership commitments
  - Early revenue: MRR/ARR growth, customer count, retention
  - Growth stage: revenue growth rate, NRR, CAC payback, cohort retention
- Customer logos or testimonials for social proof
- Key milestones on a timeline

**What to avoid**:
- Vanity metrics that don't reflect real progress (page views, downloads without retention)
- Hiding metrics — the best decks put all key numbers on one transparent slide
- Overwhelming with data — choose the 3–5 most impactful numbers
- No time dimension — metrics without trend lines are just snapshots
- Exaggerating or rounding up aggressively — investors verify in diligence

**Research finding**: 75% of early-stage rounds in 2024 went to teams showing monthly growth curves (user, retention, or revenue). Investors average 3 minutes 44 seconds per deck — the traction slide gets disproportionate attention.

**Pre-traction guidance**: If you have no revenue, this slide still exists. Show product milestones, user feedback, pilot program results, advisor endorsements, waitlist growth. Label it honestly — investors respect early-stage honesty more than inflated numbers.

## 9. Growth Flywheel Slide (Studio Signature)

**Purpose**: Show how the business scales with compounding advantages. This answers: "Why does this business get harder to compete with over time?" It connects business model, GTM choices, and defensible moats into a self-reinforcing loop.

**What great looks like**:
- Circular/cyclical diagram with 3–5 nodes forming a closed loop
- Moats annotated on the connections between nodes (data moat, network effects, switching costs, scale economies, brand/trust, ecosystem lock-in)
- Evidence that the flywheel is already turning (at least one metric proving it)
- Clear distinction between moats that exist today vs. aspirational ones
- A central callout metric: the one number that proves compounding (e.g., "NRR: 135%", "CAC declining 15% QoQ")

**What to avoid**:
- Linear funnel instead of a cycle — no feedback loop means it's a GTM slide, not a flywheel
- Too many nodes (>5) — that's a process diagram
- Claiming moats that don't exist yet without labeling them as aspirational
- No proof metric — without one, the flywheel is just a theory
- Confusing flywheel with GTM strategy

**Full framework**: See `slide-frameworks.md` → Growth Flywheel section

## 10. Competition Slide

**Purpose**: Demonstrate deep market knowledge and explain why you win. This slide reveals more about the founders than the competitors — it shows strategic thinking, market awareness, and intellectual honesty.

**What great looks like**:
- A 2×2 positioning matrix with carefully chosen axes that reflect real competitive dynamics — axes should illustrate massive trends and structural forces, not arbitrary features
- 4–6 competitors (direct, indirect, and status quo alternatives) — never claim "no competition"
- Your position in the matrix should be defensible and honest
- Headline that frames your advantage: "The only platform combining X and Y"
- Brief USP statement connecting back to the problem and solution

**Visual formats (choose one)**:
- **2×2 Matrix / Magic Quadrant**: Best for highlighting strategic positioning on two key dimensions. Choose axes that represent what competitors could never replicate.
- **Feature Comparison Table**: Best for highlighting specific feature advantages. Risk: becomes a long list without prioritization.
- **Category Map / Logo Landscape**: Best for showing different niches or clusters in the market.
- **Competitive Funnel** (like Amplitude's): Shows market layers and where you uniquely sit.

**What to avoid**:
- No competition slide — investors see this as a red flag (naive or dishonest)
- All checkmarks for you, none for competitors — "Harvey Balls" syndrome
- Axes that are rigged to make you look good but don't reflect real market dynamics
- Acknowledging competitors earlier in the deck but leaving them off this slide
- Feature-only comparison without strategic positioning

**VC perspective**: "A crowded market is one of the most common reasons investors pass. Don't let them lazy-pass on you. It's OK to be in a crowded market, but you have to be able to clear the noise and talk about why you have a market position that no one else is taking." — Richard Dulude, Underscore VC

## 11. Go-to-Market Slide

**Purpose**: Show your growth strategy — how you acquire customers and scale distribution. Unlike the flywheel (which shows compounding mechanics), this slide is about the specific channels, partnerships, and economics of customer acquisition.

**What great looks like**:
- A small number of specific, relevant channels (not a generic laundry list)
- GTM structured as a funnel: how traffic becomes leads becomes revenue
- CAC and LTV with channel-level breakdown if available
- Partnership strategy if relevant (named partners, not hypothetical)
- Near-term vs. long-term channel strategy

**What to avoid**:
- A list of every possible channel ("SEO, SEM, content, partnerships, events, social, PR, cold outreach...")
- No economics — CAC and LTV should be here or on the business model slide
- Purely aspirational partnerships ("We plan to partner with...")
- No specificity about which segment you're attacking first

## 12. Team Slide

**Purpose**: Build confidence that this team can execute. Early-stage investments are split roughly 50/50 between the idea and the team. Investors back people, not just products.

**What great looks like**:
- Founders with relevant domain expertise — what in their background makes them uniquely positioned to solve this problem?
- Key hires that fill critical gaps (CTO, VP Sales, etc.)
- Advisors with relevant industry connections
- Previous startup experience, exits, or domain credentials
- Team composition that covers the key bases: product, technology, go-to-market

**What to avoid**:
- Generic bios that could describe anyone ("Passionate entrepreneur with 10 years of experience")
- Missing key roles without acknowledging the gap
- No connection between team background and the problem being solved
- Too many people on the slide — focus on 3–5 key players

**Pro tip**: If you have notable investors or advisors already committed, include them. Existing investor participation is a strong signal.

## 13. Financials Slide

**Purpose**: Show the path — not just current numbers, but where you're going and the assumptions behind it. Investors want to see realistic projections that demonstrate you understand your business economics.

**What great looks like**:
- 12–36 month projections with key assumptions stated
- Revenue growth trajectory with clear drivers
- Path to profitability (or path to next milestone if pre-profit)
- Burn rate and runway visibility
- Key assumptions called out explicitly (not buried in fine print)

**What to avoid**:
- Hockey stick projections with no justification
- Drowning investors in spreadsheet screenshots
- No assumptions stated — every projection should have an "if X then Y" logic
- Projections that are wildly inconsistent with current traction
- Missing burn rate / runway — investors always want to know how long you have

**Guidance**: "Don't drown investors in numbers. Show direction, drivers, and realism. Explain assumptions briefly beneath the chart."

## 14. The Ask Slide

**Purpose**: Close the deal. Be specific about what you want and what it delivers. Vague asks kill momentum.

**What great looks like**:
- Specific funding amount
- Clear use of funds breakdown (product development X%, sales Y%, operations Z%)
- Milestones this funding will achieve (product launch, revenue target, team build-out)
- Timeline: when do you need the capital and how long will it last
- Optionally: round terms, existing commitments, co-investors

**What to avoid**:
- Vague: "We're looking for investment to grow"
- No use of funds breakdown
- No milestones — what does success look like 18 months post-funding?
- Asking for too wide a range ("$2M to $8M") — signals you don't know what you need

**Best close**: One confident sentence about the vision. "We're building the go-to platform for [X]."

---

## 15. Appendix

**Purpose**: Support claims without cluttering the main deck. This is for investors who want to dig deeper after the initial review.

**What to include**:
- Detailed financial model
- Technical architecture diagrams
- Extended customer case studies
- Market research sources and data
- Team bios in full
- DD report highlights (if available and appropriate to share)

The appendix should never be needed to understand the pitch — it's supplementary evidence for the interested investor.
